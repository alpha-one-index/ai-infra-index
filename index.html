What Is the AI Infrastructure Index?

The AI Infrastructure Index is a community-maintained, structured reference for AI compute infrastructure. It tracks GPU hardware specifications, cloud GPU pricing across 12 providers, MLPerf benchmark results, model VRAM sizing requirements, training cost estimates, and economic frameworks for cloud versus on-premises deployment decisions.

All data points include primary source citations. Pricing data is collected on an automated hourly schedule. GPU specification data is sourced directly from vendor datasheets (NVIDIA, AMD, Intel) and updated when vendors publish revised specifications. The methodology is documented and the collection pipeline is open source.

How Much Does Cloud GPU Computing Actually Cost?

A single H100 SXM for one hour costs between $2.00 (Nebius) and $4.10 (OCI) on-demand as of March 2026 — a 2× spread across providers. For an 8-GPU cluster (standard DGX H100 node) running for one month (720 hours), costs range from approximately $11,520/month (Nebius) to $23,616/month (OCI). Spot pricing reduces this by 25–38% on marketplaces like Vast.ai and RunPod.

How do you calculate total GPU cloud spend?

Monthly cost = (GPU count) × (on-demand $/hr) × (hours/month). A training run on 64× H100 SXM for 7 days at CoreWeave ($2.23/hr) costs approximately 64 × $2.23 × 168 = $23,975. Factor in storage I/O, egress fees, and cluster orchestration overhead (typically 5–15% of compute cost). Full breakdown methodology: specs/training-costs.md.

Which providers offer the best price-to-performance ratio for H100 inference?

At $2.00/hr for H100 SXM, Nebius currently offers the lowest cost-per-token for inference among tracked providers. CoreWeave ($2.23/hr) is preferred by many teams for its enterprise SLA and low-latency networking. Vast.ai spot at $1.80/hr provides the lowest raw cost but requires workload tolerance for preemption. For teams requiring multi-region redundancy or managed infrastructure, Azure ($2.90/hr) and Lambda Labs ($2.98/hr) add operational value at a price premium.

VRAM requirements scale linearly with parameter count and precision. A 7B parameter model in FP16 requires approximately 14 GB VRAM for inference — fitting on a single consumer GPU. A 70B parameter model needs ~140 GB in FP16, requiring either 2× H100 SXM (160 GB total) or a single AMD MI300X (192 GB). Quantization (INT8, INT4) halves or quarters VRAM at the cost of minor quality degradation.

Section 07

How Do You Reduce Cloud GPU Computing Costs?

The most impactful cost reduction strategies for GPU cloud spending are: (1) spot/preemptible instances with workload checkpointing — typically 25–40% savings; (2) quantization to reduce VRAM requirements and enable smaller/cheaper GPU selection; (3) right-sizing — selecting the minimum GPU tier that meets VRAM and throughput requirements; and (4) reserved instance commitments for predictable workloads — typically 30–50% below on-demand for 1-year terms.

What is the financial impact of GPU quantization?

Quantization reduces model precision from FP16 (2 bytes/param) to INT8 (1 byte) or INT4 (0.5 bytes). For a 70B model: FP16 needs ~140 GB VRAM (2× H100 SXM at ~$5.00/hr total) vs. INT8 ~70 GB (1× MI300X at market rate) vs. INT4 ~35 GB (1× H100 SXM at ~$2.23/hr). INT4 inference can reduce hardware costs by 50–75% vs. FP16 for the same model, with quality loss of 1–3 percentage points on most benchmarks. Techniques: GPTQ, AWQ, bitsandbytes.

When should you use spot instances for GPU workloads?

Spot instances are suitable when: (1) the workload checkpoints regularly (every 5–30 minutes for training); (2) preemption latency is acceptable (spot jobs may be interrupted with 30-second to 2-minute notice); (3) the job is not latency-sensitive or customer-facing. Avoid spot for real-time inference, jobs without checkpointing, and workloads where restarts are expensive in time or pipeline complexity. Full playbook: specs/gpu-cost-optimization-playbook.md.

Section 08

Should You Buy GPUs or Rent Cloud GPUs?

The buy-vs-rent decision depends primarily on utilization rate and time horizon. At 80–90% GPU utilization over a 3-year depreciation period, on-premises or colocation consistently delivers lower cost-per-GPU-hour than cloud on-demand pricing. Below 50–60% utilization, cloud on-demand is typically more cost-efficient due to its flexibility and zero capital expenditure. The break-even varies significantly by provider selection and local power cost.

What does a full buy vs. rent break-even analysis include?

The complete TCO calculation for on-premises or colocation includes: hardware purchase price ($25,000–$35,000/H100 SXM as of 2026), power consumption (700W × 24h × 365d × $0.08–$0.15/kWh ≈ $490–$920/year/GPU), cooling overhead (typically 1.3–1.6× power usage effectiveness), networking (InfiniBand or 400GbE switches), colocation or data center fees, and engineering staff time. A detailed framework with worked examples is in specs/buy-vs-rent-decision-framework.md.

Frequently Asked Questions About AI Infrastructure

15 common questions about GPU hardware, cloud pricing, model sizing, and cost optimization — answered with specific data points.

What is the cheapest cloud provider for H100 SXM GPUs?

As of March 2026, Nebius offers H100 SXM at $2.00/GPU/hour, the lowest tracked on-demand price. Fluidstack ($2.21/hr) and CoreWeave ($2.23/hr) are close alternatives. For spot pricing, Vast.ai ($1.80/hr) and RunPod ($1.89/hr) are lower, but require workload tolerance for preemption. OCI is the most expensive tracked provider at $4.10/hr.

The 2× price spread between Nebius and OCI means workload placement decisions can halve GPU cloud spend without any other optimization. Full comparison: specs/cloud-gpu-pricing.md.

How much VRAM do you need to run a 70B parameter LLM?

A 70B parameter model requires approximately 140 GB VRAM in FP16 (2 bytes × 70B params). Minimum configurations: 2× H100 SXM (160 GB total) or 1× AMD MI300X (192 GB). With INT8 quantization, VRAM drops to ~70 GB, fitting on a single MI300X. INT4 quantization reduces it to ~35 GB, fitting on one H100 SXM.

Training a 70B model requires roughly 4–6× the inference VRAM (optimizer states, gradients, activations), typically requiring 8–16× H100 SXM or equivalent. See full sizing guide: specs/model-gpu-sizing.md.

What is the difference between H100 SXM and H100 PCIe?

The NVIDIA H100 SXM uses the SXM5 form factor with NVLink 4.0 providing 900 GB/s GPU-to-GPU bandwidth. The H100 PCIe connects via PCIe 5.0 (128 GB/s). Memory bandwidth: SXM = 3.35 TB/s, PCIe = 2.0 TB/s. FP16 TFLOPS: SXM = 1,979, PCIe = 1,513. Price: PCIe is typically 20–30% cheaper per hour than SXM on the same platform.

SXM is strongly preferred for multi-GPU training where NVLink enables efficient gradient synchronization. PCIe is common for single-GPU inference deployments where the bandwidth premium doesn't justify the cost. Source: NVIDIA H100 datasheet.

How does the AMD MI300X compare to the NVIDIA H100 SXM?

AMD MI300X advantage: 192 GB HBM3 VRAM (2.4× H100's 80 GB) and 5.3 TB/s bandwidth (vs. 3.35 TB/s). NVIDIA H100 advantage: 1,979 FP16 TFLOPS (vs. MI300X's 1,307) and the broader CUDA/PyTorch ecosystem. MI300X is the preferred platform for serving large models (70B+ parameters) in FP16 without tensor parallelism. H100 is generally preferred for training due to NVLink scaling, FP8 support, and CUDA toolchain maturity. AMD ROCm support has improved significantly in 2025–2026 but trails CUDA in framework integration depth.

Source: AMD MI300X product page, GPU specs comparison.

Which cloud providers offer spot or preemptible GPU pricing for H100s?

Among tracked providers, Vast.ai offers H100 spot at ~$1.80/GPU/hour and RunPod at ~$1.89/GPU/hour as of March 2026 — representing a 27–38% discount vs. on-demand rates. Spot instances typically provide 30-second to 2-minute preemption notice. Providers including Nebius, CoreWeave, Lambda Labs, and OCI do not currently list public H100 SXM spot pricing.

Spot is appropriate for checkpointed training jobs, batch inference pipelines, and data preprocessing. It is not appropriate for real-time, customer-facing inference or uncheckpointed long runs. Full playbook: gpu-cost-optimization-playbook.md.

Why does NVLink matter for training large AI models?

NVLink provides direct GPU-to-GPU communication bandwidth that far exceeds PCIe. NVLink 4.0 (H100 DGX) provides 900 GB/s bidirectional bandwidth vs. PCIe 5.0's 128 GB/s — a 7× difference. NVLink 5.0 (B200/GB200) doubles this to 1.8 TB/s. In large-model training, GPUs exchange gradient tensors every step; at PCIe speeds this becomes a bottleneck above ~13B parameters in tensor-parallel configurations.

NVLink enables tensor parallelism and pipeline parallelism at the scale required for training 70B+ parameter models efficiently. Without it, gradient communication overhead dominates training time. See: specs/networking-interconnects.md.

How much faster is the NVIDIA B200 compared to the H100?

The NVIDIA B200 (Blackwell) delivers 4,500 FP16 TFLOPS — 2.3× the H100 SXM's 1,979 TFLOPS. FP8 TFLOPS: 9,000 (B200) vs. 3,958 (H100 SXM). Memory bandwidth: 8.0 TB/s (B200) vs. 3.35 TB/s (H100 SXM) — a 2.4× improvement. VRAM: 192 GB HBM3e (B200) vs. 80 GB HBM3 (H100 SXM). TDP increases from 700W (H100) to 1,000W (B200). The GB200 NVL72 superchip reaches 9,000 FP16 TFLOPS, 16.0 TB/s bandwidth, and 384 GB VRAM.

Source: NVIDIA B200 datasheet.

At what point does buying GPUs beat renting them from the cloud?

At 80–90% GPU utilization over a 3-year depreciation period, on-premises or colocation typically beats cloud on-demand on cost-per-GPU-hour. An H100 SXM card at $30,000 with CoreWeave on-demand at $2.23/hr implies break-even at ~13,450 GPU-hours — approximately 18–19 months of continuous use. Full TCO includes power ($0.08–$0.15/kWh × 700W), cooling (PUE 1.3–1.6×), networking, colocation fees, and staff.

Below 50–60% utilization, cloud on-demand is generally more cost-efficient due to zero capex and flexibility. See the full framework: buy-vs-rent-decision-framework.md.

What is the best GPU for inference versus training?

For inference: AMD MI300X (192 GB, 5.3 TB/s) is preferred for large-model inference due to VRAM capacity. NVIDIA H200 (141 GB, 4.8 TB/s) is a strong alternative with better CUDA tooling. For throughput-optimized inference, H100 SXM with FP8 (3,958 TFLOPS) offers strong tokens/second per dollar on models fitting in 80 GB.

For training: NVIDIA H100 SXM and B200 are preferred due to NVLink multi-GPU scaling, mature CUDA/PyTorch/Megatron-LM toolchain, and FP8 native support. AMD MI300X is increasingly viable for training via ROCm, but ecosystem depth trails CUDA for production at scale. See: specs/inference-benchmarks.md.

How can you reduce GPU cloud costs by 30–50%?

Five strategies that routinely achieve 30–50% cost reductions: (1) Multi-cloud arbitrage — Nebius at $2.00/hr vs. OCI at $4.10/hr is a 51% saving for equivalent H100 SXM. (2) Spot instances — Vast.ai spot at $1.80/hr vs. on-demand is 27% off. (3) Reserved/committed pricing — 1-year commits typically save 30–50% vs. on-demand on providers that offer them. (4) Quantization — INT8 can allow downgrading from 2× H100 to 1× MI300X for 70B inference, potentially halving cost. (5) Dynamic batching — improving GPU utilization from 40% to 80% effectively halves cost-per-inference.

Full playbook with implementation notes: gpu-cost-optimization-playbook.md.

What is quantization and how does it reduce GPU memory requirements?

Quantization reduces the numerical precision of model weights: FP32 = 4 bytes, FP16/BF16 = 2 bytes, INT8 = 1 byte, INT4 = 0.5 bytes. A 70B model: FP16 ≈ 140 GB → INT8 ≈ 70 GB → INT4 ≈ 35 GB. Common techniques: GPTQ (post-training weight quantization), AWQ, bitsandbytes. FP8 inference is natively supported on NVIDIA H100 and B200, delivering up to 2× throughput vs. FP16 with minimal quality loss for most generation tasks.

Quality impact: INT8 typically shows <1% degradation on common benchmarks; INT4 shows 1–3% degradation. For production inference, INT8 is generally considered safe; INT4 requires task-specific evaluation. Source: gpu-cost-optimization-playbook.md.

How do Google TPUs compare to NVIDIA GPUs for AI training?

Google TPU v5p delivers 459 TFLOPS (BF16) per chip and scales to multi-thousand-chip pods via proprietary ICI interconnect on Google Cloud. NVIDIA H100 SXM delivers 1,979 FP16 TFLOPS per chip with broad CUDA/PyTorch ecosystem support. TPUs offer better cost-efficiency for JAX-native transformer training on GCP, particularly at Google-scale pod configurations. GPUs are more flexible, supporting a wider range of frameworks, custom CUDA kernels, and non-Transformer architectures.

AWS Trainium2 is an analogous purpose-built option for AWS/PyTorch workloads. For most teams outside Google and AWS, NVIDIA GPU clusters remain the dominant choice due to ecosystem breadth. See: specs/ai-accelerators.md.

How much does it cost to train a GPT-4 scale AI model?

GPT-4 class models (estimated ~1T parameters, mixture-of-experts) are estimated to have cost $50M–$100M in compute at 2022–2023 A100 pricing. At current H100 SXM rates (~$2.50/hr on-demand), a comparable training run would cost $30M–$80M. Smaller models: Llama 3 8B ≈ <$500K; Llama 3 70B ≈ $2M–$5M; Llama 3 405B ≈ $10M–$20M. These estimates assume efficient GPU utilization (60–80%) and exclude engineering, data pipeline, and infrastructure overhead, which commonly add 1.5–3× to the pure compute cost.

Detailed training cost estimates with methodology: specs/training-costs.md.

What are the main frameworks used for training large AI models in 2026?

Primary frameworks for large-scale training: PyTorch (dominant for research and most production training, with FSDP and DDP for distributed training); JAX (Google/DeepMind, pairs with XLA compiler and TPUs); Megatron-LM (NVIDIA's large transformer training framework with tensor/pipeline parallelism); DeepSpeed (Microsoft, ZeRO optimizer for memory efficiency); Hugging Face Accelerate (wrapper abstracting distributed backends).

Most large model training uses PyTorch + Megatron-LM or PyTorch + DeepSpeed on H100/A100 clusters. JAX is standard for Google-internal and TPU-based training. ROCm provides AMD GPU support for PyTorch and JAX, though integration depth lags CUDA.
